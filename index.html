Overview

Executive Summary

This framework answers a core question: how should we allocate ad spend to maximise the campaign's impact? We have built a data rubric identifying which demographic and psychographic datapoints define our target audience, estimated its scale, and used county-level scoring to pinpoint where our dollars will spend most efficiently.

Why we did this

Too many naturally progressive voters are considering voting against Democratic candidates - or not voting at all. The political climate has become so fractured that people are willing to cut off their nose to spite their face. Previously, mobilising the base was considered "preaching to the choir". Now there is no choir. Without an evidence-based targeting model, ad dollars get spread too thin or concentrated on intuition. This framework ensures spend goes only to the counties with the highest concentration of our target audience.

What we built

Two county-level scoring models using publicly available data.
Model A: Where Democratic voters are disappearing.
Model B: Where trans rights are actively contested.
Counties scoring high on both = top priority for ad spend.

What it delivers

  • A prioritised list of target counties deployable on social media platforms
  • Four sub-audience segments with tailored content and targeting strategies
  • A data source catalogue identifying what to buy, what is free, and what to request
  • Clear next steps to move from framework to live campaign

Total Addressable Audience

Four Sub-Audience Segments

◆ Purity Progressives

4–6M

Voted Dem but feel left behind ideologically. Prioritises purity over pragmatism. Votes third-party, writes in a candidate, or stays home as a deliberate statement. College-educated, urban, 25–45.

Gallup: 45% of liberal Dems want party further left. 15–20% of 18.2M non-voter pool = 2.7–3.6M base; scaled to 4–6M including third-party votes.

◆ Exhausted Moderates

10–14M

Not ideologically alienated, just tuned out. Overwhelmed by political noise, no longer believes their vote matters. Were reliable Dem voters; now simply not showing up. Suburban, 35–55, working families.

Gallup 37% Dem favourability (early 2025, historically low). CPS: 6.8M cited disengagement (presidential floor); midterm pool 2–3x larger implies 10–14M. Pew: 8.7M cited apathy; 57% don't wish they had voted.

◆ Single-Issue Defectors

3–5M

Voted Dem in 2020, disengaged over a single failure: cost of living (31%), healthcare, housing. Still progressive on most issues, not conservative converts. The hardest segment to identify without voter file data.

Lake Research (2020 Biden → 2024 non-voter panel): ~15–25% of non-voter pool. CPS: 14.7% cited candidate/issue dislike = 2.7M presidential floor; rises in midterms.

◆ Disengaged Young Voters

8–12M

Progressive in values, disengaged from institutions. Votes when motivated, disappears in midterms. Many never registered. Highest potential ROI per contact: strong Dem lean, long voting horizon, responsive to cause-based messaging.

CIRCLE/Tufts 24pt presidential-to-midterm gap × 30M 18–29s = ~7.2M structural non-voters; scaled to 8–12M for Dem-leaning subset. CPS: 2.6M registered 18–24s skipped the presidential. Competitive states: 31% youth midterm vs 47% presidential (CIRCLE 2022). Pew: young adults grew 25%→30% of all non-voters 2020→2024.

Pool basis: Census CPS Nov 2024: 18.2M registered non-voters validates ~19M estimate  ·  Pipeline: 7.5M fewer Dem votes 2024 vs 2020 (hard floor)  ·  Segments overlap; estimates are directional

Model A

The Disengagement Index

"Where are Democratic voters disappearing - and where does their absence hurt most?"

Every US county receives a score from 0-100 based on four weighted variables.

Four inputs: Turnout Decline (35%) + Competitive Race (30%) + Young Voter Density (20%) + Progressive Organising Capacity (15%)

Qualifying filter: Only counties with >=30% Democratic vote share (2024 presidential) are included. Below that threshold, insufficient progressive base to justify ad spend.

Model A - Variable 1

Democratic Turnout Decline

Weight
35%

What it measures

Change in Democratic votes cast across recent election cycles. Counties with the steepest decline score highest.

Calculation

Average of two decline metrics:

Presidential: (Dem 2024 - Dem 2020) / Dem 2020
Midterm: (Dem 2022 - Dem 2018) / Dem 2018

Data Source

MIT Election Data + Science Lab (MEDSL) - county-level returns. Presidential 2020 & 2024, Congressional 2018 & 2022. Freely available. Normalised to 0-100 via min-max scaling.

Model A - Variable 2

Competitive 2026 Race

Weight
30%
Race RatingScore
Toss-Up (Senate / House / Governor)100
Lean D or Lean R75
Likely D or Likely R25
Safe D or Safe R0

If multiple competitive races, highest-scoring race is used.

Key 2026 Toss-Up Races

Senate: North Carolina, Michigan, Maine, Ohio (special)

House: 18+ seats - AZ-01, CO-08, ME-02, MI-07, OH-09, OH-13, PA-07, PA-08, PA-10, and more

Governor: Arizona, Georgia, Michigan, Wisconsin, Nevada, Iowa

Sources: Cook Political Report, Sabato's Crystal Ball

Model A - Variable 3

Young Voter Density (18-34)

Weight
20%

The 24-Point Gap

47%
Youth turnout, 2024 presidential CIRCLE / Tufts
→
~23%
Youth turnout, 2022 midterm CIRCLE / Tufts

Roughly half of young presidential-year voters don't show up for midterms - the single largest source of progressive vote loss.

Methodology

Calculation: (Pop aged 18-34) / (Total voting-age pop) per county, normalised to 0-100.

Data: Census ACS Table B01001 - Sex by Age. County-level, annual updates, freely available.

Majorities of Gen Z and Millennials now identify as political independents (Gallup 2025), reinforcing the disengagement signal.

Model A - Variable 4

Progressive Organising Capacity

Weight
15%

What it measures

Small-dollar ActBlue donations ($5-$200) per capita by county, normalised to 0-100. Drawn from FEC itemised receipts filed at FEC.gov.

Calculation: (ActBlue unique donor count) / (Registered voter count) per county, min-max scaled to 0-100.

Data: FEC.gov electronic filings. Free. Updated quarterly. Mapped from zip to county via ZCTA-to-county crosswalk.

Rationale

Small-dollar donation activity is a direct proxy for progressive civic engagement - people motivated enough to give $10 to a campaign but who may not translate that motivation into midterm votes.

Counties with high donor density but low turnout represent the highest-value mobilisation targets - latent progressive energy waiting to be activated.

Excludes donations >$200 to focus on grassroots giving patterns rather than high-net-worth individual donors.

Model B - Variable 3

Progressive Voter Density

Weight
25%

A composite of two equal sub-signals - each weighted 50% within this variable's 25% contribution to the overall score.

Sub-signal 1: Democratic Vote Share (50%)

Calculation: (Dem votes 2024) / (Total votes 2024) per county, normalised to 0-100.

Data: MIT MEDSL county-level presidential returns 2024. Free.

Ensures trans salience is only elevated in counties with a reachable progressive base. A county with a ballot measure but 85% Republican vote share is not a productive place to spend.

Sub-signal 2: PRRI Acceptance Score (50%)

Source: PRRI American Values Atlas - annual metro and state-level survey of LGBTQ+ acceptance attitudes across the US.

Use: Higher acceptance scores identify counties where pro-trans messaging is more likely to land and where progressive voters operate in a socially supportive environment. Applied at metro/state level, interpolated to county.

Free. Annual release. Covers all 50 states and 30+ major metros.

Sneak Peek: Early Signals from the Model

Full analysis is in progress. Here is what the initial output is already showing clearly.

Finding 1

The Rio Grande Valley stands alone at the top

Hidalgo, Cameron, Willacy and Kleberg counties in South Texas lead both models simultaneously, the rarest and highest-confidence signal. These heavily Latino border communities swung 15-20 points toward Republicans in 2024 while still clearing 43-48% Democratic. Disengagement is acute and recent. Texas has the highest trans issue salience score nationally.

Model A scores: 62-73  ·  Model B scores: 82-83

Finding 2

Iowa's 49 counties need a second look before committing budget

Iowa has more Tier 1 counties than any other state, driven by sharp and widespread 2020→2024 Dem decline. But Iowa has been shifting right structurally for a decade. The model reads this as recoverable disengagement, but it may be permanent partisan realignment. The RGV swing is two years old and localised; Iowa's is a decade in the making. This is the most important validation question before the next planning stage.

Recommend: qualitative research in 2-3 Iowa counties before budget allocation

Finding 3

North Carolina is the most confident large-scale opportunity

38 Tier 1 counties, concentrated in the rural east among majority-Black communities with documented Dem disengagement. A genuinely competitive Senate race and multiple toss-up House districts mean electoral stakes are real. Unlike Iowa, NC's decline is recent and geographically concentrated, more consistent with with the recoverable disengagement the model is designed to find.

Both models point here: real disengagement data + verified electoral competition

Further analysis underway: precinct-level validation, qualitative research design for Iowa, and Tier 2 county prioritisation within NC and TX. Full county rankings available on request.

Three Sourcing Gaps: What They Mean

Most planned sources loaded cleanly. Three required workarounds that affect how to read Model B.

ACLU Anti-Trans Bill Tracker

JavaScript-rendered; bill volumes by state could not be scraped.

Workaround: MAP ban/shield classification used as structural proxy. Within-state differentiation is approximated from published research.

PRRI American Values Atlas

JavaScript web app; no API, no bulk download, no scrape path.

Workaround: State-level acceptance scores drawn from published PRRI research. Directionally correct; not precisely sourced.

ALA Book Challenge Data

Published as national PDF infographics only; no geographic breakdown exists.

Workaround: County modifier dropped from model. All counties in a state share the same trans activity score.

Limitations & Assumptions

Model Limitations

"Disaffection" is an attitude, not a demographic

Turnout decline is a proxy - it captures people who moved, died, or changed registration, not just those who chose not to vote. Mitigation: Cross-reference with voter file data (L2/TargetSmart) to distinguish deregistration from non-voting.

Model B's lead variable is state-level

All counties in a state receive the same "trans issue activity" score. County-level differentiation relies on the other two variables. This is acceptable - Model B's value is in state prioritisation.

Race ratings change throughout the cycle

A "Toss-Up" in May 2026 may be "Lean" by October. Mitigation: Re-run the model quarterly and before any major budget allocation decision.

Third-party vote data is incomplete

Many third-party votes aren't broken out by county. Limits ability to identify "Purity Progressive" geography through past voting behaviour. Use Green Party state-level results as a directional proxy.

Limitations & Assumptions

Key Assumptions

1

The 30% Dem vote share qualifying filter is appropriate. This excludes deep-red counties. If the campaign's goal evolves to include persuasion of moderates (not just progressive mobilisation), the filter should be lowered.

2

Midterm turnout patterns from 2018 and 2022 are predictive of 2026. Standard assumption in political targeting but could be disrupted by unprecedented events.

3

The four sub-audience segments are meaningful and distinct enough for different creative approaches. Should be validated through early-campaign A/B creative testing by geography.

Model Augmentation & Validation

Emerging & Unconventional Data Sources

Non-traditional signals to strengthen geographic prioritisation and independently validate county scores.

Drag Venue Density

Count of active drag bars, cabarets and LGBTQ+ venues by county (DragDB.com, Google Maps API, Yelp). High venue density signals visible queer community presence and elevated anti-trans legislative targeting. Free (scrape)

Pride Event Growth Rate

Year-on-year change in Pride events by county (InterPride / Eventbrite). Rapid growth signals an energising community: a leading indicator of both salience and mobilisation potential. Free

Local News Coverage Volume

Article count on transgender issues by DMA from Media Cloud (mediacloud.org). Counties with disproportionate news volume have a primed electorate and a media environment that amplifies campaign messaging. Free API

IRS Migration Flows

County-to-county migration by year (IRS Statistics of Income). Inbound migrants from high-legislation states (TX, FL, TN) into our Tier 1 geographies are likely to be trans-issue-aware and politically motivated. Free

Gender-Affirming Care Provider Density

Count of gender-affirming healthcare providers by county (GLMA directory). Where providers are concentrated, so are patients, families, and informed advocates primed to engage. Free

How to use these signals

Run as independent checks against the primary model output. A county scoring high on Models A + B and registering on 2-3 of these emerging indicators has the strongest confidence for Tier 1 designation and the highest budget priority.
